**Case Study: The "Wrong Transfer" UPI Scam**

*Fictional scenario based on widely documented fraud patterns in India*

**What is it?**

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) fraud is a social-engineering attack where criminals manipulate victims into authorising payments — or revealing sensitive credentials — under false pretences. Unlike hacking, the victim is tricked into doing the criminal's work for them.

**How it typically happens:**

 The step-by-step anatomy of this scam:
1. Initial Contact: The fraudster contacts the victim via phone, SMS, or messaging apps, often posing as a bank representative or government official.

2. Deception: They claim there is a problem with the victim’s account or that they are eligible for a cashback/refund, prompting urgent action.

3. Trick into Sharing Details: Victims are asked to share their UPI PIN, OTP, or to authorize a payment through a fake link.

4. Execution: Once the attacker has the necessary credentials, they transfer money from the victim’s account to their own, often in small installments to avoid detection.

5. Covering Tracks: Fraudsters may delete transaction alerts or use multiple accounts to launder the stolen funds.

 **Who is targeted?**

First-time UPI users, elderly individuals, and small business owners are most commonly targeted — particularly those unfamiliar with the distinction between a "collect request" (which debits your account) and receiving a payment.

**Consequences**

For the victim, consequences include immediate financial loss (often ₹10,000–₹2,00,000), psychological distress, and difficult recovery prospects since UPI transactions are irreversible. Legally, such offences fall under **Section 66C and 66D of the IT Act, 2000** and relevant sections of the **Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita**. Criminals face up to 3 years imprisonment and fines. However, low conviction rates persist because funds are quickly routed through layered "mule" accounts before tracing is possible.

**Preventive tip:** Your UPI PIN should *only* be entered when *sending* money — never to "receive" it. A collect request always debits your account.

**Conclusion:**

UPI fraud demonstrates the risks of digital payments when combined with social engineering. Awareness and cautious behavior are the most effective preventive measures.


*Source: National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal (cybercrime.gov.in); RBI consumer awareness bulletins.*
